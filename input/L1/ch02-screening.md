## Section 2.1 — Basic Concepts (pp. 20–22)

### 2.1.1 Definitions: screening vs. early diagnosis

**Summary.** The source carefully distinguishes *screening* (proactive testing
of an asymptomatic target population to identify pre-cancerous emergent disease)
from *early diagnosis* (clinical workup of an already symptomatic individual to
detect cancer at an early stage). Screening is framed as a *system* — registry,
recall-callback, follow-up — not just a test.

> **English translation:**
>
> *Screening (Скрининг):* From among the apparently healthy population without
> specific signs or symptoms, identify the high-risk subgroup, and through
> specific, simple, accessible test(s) administered as part of an examination
> service, identify pre-cancerous emergent disease, and through diagnosis,
> treatment, and monitoring conducted in a sustainable, continuous, integrated
> system, organize systematic activity. This is screening.
>
> *Early diagnosis:* For individuals with possible cancer signs or symptoms,
> diagnose at the earliest stage possible and initiate timely treatment in a
> sustainable, continuous, integrated activity. Early diagnosis has three
> essential components: (1) information from cancer signs/symptoms and
> clinical care; (2) clinical assessment, diagnosis, and cancer-stage
> determination; (3) timely initiation of treatment.

### 2.1.3 Test-quality criteria: sensitivity, specificity, acceptability

**Summary.** Standard screening-test quality definitions, applied to cervical
cancer and cervical pre-cancer (УХЭ).

> **English translation:**
>
> *Sensitivity:* The proportion of women with cervical cancer (УХХХ) or
> pre-cancer (ХУЭ) for whom the test correctly returns a positive result. Lower
> sensitivity means a smaller proportion of disease-bearing women are detected
> — hence not detected, hence not treated.
>
> *Specificity:* The proportion of healthy women for whom the test correctly
> returns a negative result. Lower specificity means healthy women are
> incorrectly diagnosed and undergo unnecessary treatment.
>
> *Acceptability:* How acceptable the test is to the woman undergoing it and
> to the healthcare provider performing it.

### 2.1.4 Three-tier prevention model

**Summary.** Cervical cancer prevention follows the standard three-tier model
(primary, secondary, tertiary). Primary prevention reduces HPV infection
(vaccination, education); secondary prevention detects and treats pre-cancer;
tertiary prevention reduces cervical-cancer mortality.

> **English translation:**
>
> 30–50% of cancer is preventable. Prevention is the most cost-effective and
> long-term cancer-control approach. Prevention's purpose is to call people to
> healthy lifestyles, raise health-literacy levels, and reduce risk behaviors.
>
> Cervical cancer prevention proceeds along three lines, like other cancers:
>
> 1. **Primary prevention** focuses on reducing HPV infection. Activities:
>    - HPV vaccination of girls aged 9–13 before sexual activity onset (see Ch. 4)
>    - Provide health education on cervical-cancer prevention (see Ch. 3)
>    - Adhere to the ABC principle (A = Abstain, B = Be faithful, C = use Condom)
>
> 2. **Secondary prevention** focuses on detecting, treating, and monitoring
>    cervical pre-cancer (ХУЭ). Activities:
>    - Provide diagnosis-and-treatment counseling and disseminate information
>    - Include target-age women in screening
>    - Fully treat and monitor every detected case of pre-cancer
>
> 3. **Tertiary prevention** focuses on reducing cervical-cancer mortality.
>    Activities:
>    - Diagnose cervical cancer at the earliest possible stage
>    - Provide stage-appropriate treatment for each stage
>    - Provide palliative-care services
>    - Refer cervical-cancer patients between facility tiers as appropriate

### 2.2.1 Target age groups and frequency

**Summary.** A/641 specifies two distinct target-age regimens, selected by
local laboratory capability:
- HPV-incapable units: cytology at ages 30, 33, 36, 39, 42, 45, 48, 51, 54, 57, 60.
- HPV-capable units: HPV high-risk-type test at ages 35 and 45.
- Pregnant women in their first trimester are opportunistically included.

universally available, cytology is the fallback. It is not framed as a
permanent parallel regime.

>
> Target population for cervical cancer screening, frequency:
>
> Target age group:
> - In administrative units that lack the capability to perform HPV testing,

> - In units capable of performing HPV testing, women aged 35 and 45 will
>   undergo HPV high-risk-type screening.

### 2.2.5 HPV arm (Зураг 2.4, p. 24)

**Summary.** HPV pathway: take both HPV sample and a cytology sample
simultaneously at age 35 or 45. Send HPV sample to lab; store cytology slide
unread. If HPV negative → exit (or re-screen at 45 if age 35). If HPV positive
→ read the stored cytology slide. If cytology negative → repeat HPV in 1 year;
if positive on repeat → colposcopy. If cytology ≥ ASC-US → colposcopy. From
colposcopy → biopsy or endocervical curettage. Histology routes to CIN
protocol or invasive-cancer pathway (form АМ-5).

The algorithm uses **ЭИМ-5.1** at primary care (sample collection, repeat HPV)
and **ЭИМ-5.2** at secondary care (colposcopy, biopsy).

> **English translation (figure 2.4 contents):**
>
> Take HPV high-risk-type detection test and cytology sample. [ЭИМ-5.1]
>   - Store the cytology slide; send the HPV sample to the laboratory.
>
> HPV negative:
>   - All test results — negative
>   - Age 35 client → undergo HPV high-risk type detection test at age 45
>   - Age 45 client → remove from screening registry
>
> HPV positive:
>   - Examine the stored cytology slide. [ЭИМ-5.1]
>     - Negative → Repeat HPV high-risk-type detection test 1 year later [ЭИМ-5.1]

### 2.3.1 Registry information flow architecture (Зураг 2.5, p. 26)

**Summary.** ⚠️ **Currently missing from L2.** A/641 establishes a hierarchical
registry with three tiers:

2. **Secondary care tier** — aimag and district integrated hospitals

and a "recall system" side (outgoing reminders, callback triggers).

### 2.3.5 Yearly target-population registration (p. 28)

**Summary.** Each year, the population information bank selects women in the
relevant age cohorts; an invitation is sent 7 days before the appointment;
the woman is to be screened within 30 days of the invitation date.

> **English translation:**
>
> 1. Registering target-age population, delivering invitations, reminding:
>
> Mongolian-citizen women aged 30 and older — link information from health
> centers via the cancer-screening electronic registry to create a population
> information bank.
>
> - Each year, from this population information bank, women aged 35 and 45
>   AND women aged 30, 33, 36, 39, 42, 45, 48, 51, 54, 57, 60 are selected
>   and registered.
> - Deliver the screening invitation to the woman 7 days before the
>   appointment date.
> - Include the woman in screening within 30 days of the invitation date.
> - Pregnant women in their first 3 months of pregnancy are included in
>   opportunistic screening.

## Translator's notes (Claude, May 2026)

1. **"Бэлтгэлийн хугацаанд" (preparatory period).** This phrase in the
   target-age section (p. 22) is the strongest signal that the cytology arm
   is framed as transitional, not permanent. I considered alternative
   readings ("ready period," "scheduled period") but the policy direction
   stated elsewhere (build HPV capability nationwide) makes "preparatory
   period" the most coherent reading. Worth verification by a Mongolian
   medical translator.

2. **Form-naming systems.** It took me ~30 minutes to reconcile the two
   coexisting systems (5.1/5.2 in figures vs. 5а..5е in Table 2.1). I am
   confident in the resolution: 5.1 and 5.2 are *summary patient records* at
   primary and secondary care tiers; the Cyrillic-letter forms are
   *accompanying referral/result/recall slips*. The figures key data-flow
   steps to the relevant summary record (5.1 or 5.2). The catalog table
   lists which slips circulate at each tier. Both systems are in use and
   neither is wrong.

3. **Birthday-anchored scheduling.** Section 2.3.5 reveals that screening
   invitations are tied to the woman's birthday in the relevant year (7 days
   before, then a 30-day window after). I did not anticipate this. It is
   L2-relevant if a future PlanDefinition encodes "due-on-birthday" semantics.

4. **Хүснэгт 2.2 vs. Annex 4 Table X.4.** These are two different
   coding tables. Хүснэгт 2.2 (Ch. 2, p. 27) is the screening-visit ICD-10
   coding (Z01.4 / Z12.4 vs. N86 / N87.x / N88.x / D06 / C53.x). Annex 4 Table
   X.4 — which I have not yet read — is reportedly the Bethesda × CIN cross-tab
   used by cytology and histology labs. The current L2 conflates them.

5. **"бэлгийн харьцаанд оролгоосын өмнө" (before sexual-activity onset)**
   language for the HPV vaccination age 9–13 (Ch. 2.1.4) is socially specific
   and worth preserving in any English-language community-facing materials.
